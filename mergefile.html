Necessity of Insurance Policy

According to the Motor Vehicle Act 1988, Section 146, insurance is compulsory for all vehicles running on the road. Motor insurance provides protection to the vehicle owner against (i) damages to his/her vehicle and (ii) pays for any Third Party Liability determined as per law against the owner of the vehicle. Third Party Insurance is a statutory requirement. The vehicle owner is legally liable for any injury or damage to third-party life or property caused by or arising out of the use of the vehicle in a public place.

Transfer of Insurance Policy

1. On transferring the vehicle, the transfer of insurance is compulsory within 14 days. If the insurance is not transferred within 14 days, then the insurance policy is not valid. The insurance must be in the name of the vehicle owner.

2. On vehicle transfer, the policy can be transferred to the new vehicle owner, but NCB is not given to them. The new vehicle owner has to pay the difference of NCB for the remaining time period. Then the insurance policy transfers to the new owner.

3. After selling the vehicle, if the insurance holder wants, the policy can be transferred to another new vehicle, and the profit of NCB can be received.

4. On transferring, the old certificate of insurance is required to be surrendered, and a fee of Rs.50/- is to be submitted for the issue of a new certificate of insurance. If the old certificate of insurance cannot be submitted, then full details about the policy should be submitted for issuing a new certificate of insurance.

Certificate of Insurance and Cover Note

According to the Central Motor Vehicle Act, 1989, the Certificate of Insurance is a certificate issued by the insurance company under Form 51.

Whereas the Cover Note is issued under Form 52. A cover note is valid up to 60 days from the issuing date, and the policy of insurance is issued before the expiration of the cover note.

HP (Hypothecation) in Insurance

When the registered owner enters into an agreement of loan with the finance company, the name of the finance company is entered in the registration certificate (RC); this is called hypothecation. After entering the HP in the RC, the name of the financer also appears in the insurance policy.

At the time of claim receiving, the finance company has the first right on the claim money. Any remaining amount (if any) is given to the policyholder or loan bearer.

On Adding Detail to the Vehicle

According to the insurance company, after purchasing a policy, if there is any change in the vehicle, there is a procedure for changing the policy by paying an extra premium for that addition. For example, on the addition of an LPG or CNG kit to the vehicle, the RTA should be notified for a change in RC. Then the insurance company should be notified so that by paying an extra premium, that addition can be claimed.

Importance of IRDA in Insurance Sector

Role of IRDA

All insurance companies in India operate under the IRDA (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority). It regulates the insurance sector, promotes its growth, and ensures the protection of policyholders' rights.

Filing Complaints

If you are dissatisfied with an insurance company, you can file a complaint with IRDA.

1. Contact the grievance redressal officer of the company.
2. Submit a written complaint with necessary documents.
3. Obtain a written acknowledgment of the complaint with the date.
4. If unsatisfied within 15 days, escalate to IRDA.

NO CLAIM BONUS (NCB)

A) NCB is given to the insurance holder, not to the vehicle.

NCB (No Claim Bonus) is a discount given by the insurance company for not making any claim in the previous year.

B) After selling vehicle...

If the insurance holder sells the vehicle, the policy can be transferred to the new vehicle, and the NCB benefit can be retained.

C) On vehicle transfer...

The policy can be transferred to the new owner, but the NCB is not given to the new owner.

D) Transfer of NCB on changing insurance company...

Yes, the NCB benefit can be obtained from the new company upon providing proof of the NCB certificate from the old insurer.

RISKS NOT COVERED IN THESE CASES

  • If the damage occurs due to war, terror attacks, civil wars, or nuclear attacks.
  • If the policy is not valid. The policy must be renewed on time without any break. If the accident happens between the expiration of the old policy and the renewal of the new policy, the loss is not covered.
  • If the driver is driving the vehicle under the influence of alcohol or drugs, etc.
  • If the vehicle is driven by a person without a valid driving license.
  • If the damage occurs due to violation of car manufacturing guidelines.
  • If the damage occurs due to an accident done intentionally.
  • If the claim is rejected by the insurance company. The insurance holder can appeal in court within 12 months of the rejection.

RISK IS COVERED IN THESE CASES

  • By fire explosion, self-ignition, or lightning
  • By house breaking or theft
  • By riot and strike
  • By earthquake
  • By flood, typhoon, hurricane, or storm
  • By accidental exterior means
  • By malicious act
  • By landslide, or rockslide

Factors Affecting Your Insurance Premium

Insured Declared Value (IDV)

The IDV is the maximum sum assured fixed by the insurer that your insurer will provide if your vehicle is stolen or suffers total damage.

Cubic Capacity (cc of Engine)

The cubic capacity of your vehicle's engine plays a significant role in determining the premium amount. Higher cc usually means higher premium.

Geographical Zones

Your vehicle's location can affect the premium, as areas with higher risk factors (like theft or accidents) usually have higher premiums.

Vehicle Manufacturing Year

Older vehicles might attract higher premiums due to the potential for more frequent repairs, while newer vehicles could have lower premiums.
